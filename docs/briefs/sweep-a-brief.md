# Sweep A — Allocator / FoF Quant Practice

## 1. State of practice

Sophisticated allocators converge on a remarkably consistent skeleton, even though the details are proprietary. The public record — annual reports, investment-belief statements, consultant marketing, and the academic canon — supports the following as **table stakes**:

**Belief-anchored selection.** Leading funds start from an explicit prior that net-of-fee skill is rare and hard to access. NZ Super's published investment beliefs state plainly that "the ability to consistently generate excess returns from skill… (net of fees) is rare; where this ability exists, it is hard to access," and that alpha opportunities are strategy- and regime-dependent ([nzsuperfund.nz/beliefs](https://nzsuperfund.nz/how-we-invest/beliefs/)). NZ Super evaluates managers on two published axes — **Conviction** (confidence in repeatable edge, org quality/fit) and **Operational due diligence** — benchmarked against a passive Reference Portfolio ([evaluation page](https://nzsuperfund.nz/how-we-invest/investment-managers/evaluation/)). CPP Investments frames external manager selection around "people, process, performance" and repeatability of the strategy, and structures fees as a small base plus a share of value created ([CPP Investments](https://www.cppinvestments.com/insight-institute/amy-flikerski-on-the-evolving-landscape-of-external-portfolio-management/)). Norway's NBIM is unusually transparent about its external-manager program, which it uses mainly in emerging markets and small-/mid-cap segments where specialist bottom-up stock-pickers can add value. Its retrospective *Investing with External Managers: The 20-Year History* covers 1999–2018 and reports a gross excess return of "more than 2 percent annually," of which about four-fifths was retained after fees ([NBIM 20-year history, PDF](https://www.nbim.no/contentassets/62a6c07fb01641e3a6ab7fe27f23de69/investing-with-external-managers_the-20-year-history.pdf)). NBIM's current disclosures update and extend these numbers: as of end-2025 it ran 111 externally managed mandates across 103 organisations, and it reports an average annual excess return after costs of 1.7 percent, with external managers beating their benchmarks in 25 of the 28 years since the first mandates were awarded in 1998 — cumulatively about 93 billion kroner after costs ([NBIM external mandates](https://www.nbim.no/en/investments/external-mandates/); [NBIM Annual Report 2024](https://www.nbim.no/en/news-and-insights/reports/2024/annual-report-2024/web-report-annual-report-2024/)). <!-- QC follow-up: original brief misstated this as ~24 bps over 1998–2024; corrected against NBIM primary sources --> The Future Fund runs 100+ external managers with an explicit constraint against single-manager concentration and regular reviews ([Future Fund](https://www.futurefund.gov.au/en/investment/how-we-invest/investment-managers)).

**Factor decomposition as the analytical core.** The Fung–Hsieh seven-factor model (equity, size, two rate/credit factors, three trend-following straddle factors) is the workhorse for separating hedge-fund alpha from alternative beta; it explains up to ~80% of diversified hedge-fund index variance ([Fung–Hsieh 2004, PDF](https://people.duke.edu/~dah7/HF-RF.pdf); [factor data library](https://people.duke.edu/~dah7/HFRFData.htm)). Practitioner allocators explicitly run factor regressions to test whether "alpha" survives once equity, credit, rates, and vol exposures are stripped out, treating confidence intervals that cross zero as a warning sign.

**Consultant model.** Albourne, Aksia, Mercer, and Cambridge Associates all pair investment due diligence (IDD) with operational due diligence (ODD) at fund/manager/portfolio levels. Cambridge screens ~11,000 managers down to ~100–125 recommendations ([Cambridge Associates](https://www.cambridgeassociates.com/hedge-funds/)). Mercer rates strategies (A / B+ / etc.) against four pillars — idea generation, portfolio construction, implementation, business management — via a research-note-plus-ratings-committee process ([Mercer](https://www.mercer.com/en-us/solutions/investments/wealth-managers/investment-manager-research/)). Albourne is subscription-based and covers risk at asset/fund/manager/portfolio levels ([Albourne services](https://www-us.albourne.com/albourne/services)); Aksia's ODD is investigative, probing alignment, conflicts, and culture ([Aksia](https://www.aksia.com/research-coverage/)).

**Risk aggregation under tiered transparency.** The industry standard for exactly the problem this team faces — combining position-light and position-rich managers — is **Open Protocol** (OPERA), a standardized risk template with three drill-down levels (portfolio-level stress tests → asset class → sector), now covering >$1trn AUM and overseen by the SBAI with Albourne ([theopenprotocol.org](https://theopenprotocol.org/); [SBAI](https://www.sbai.org/toolbox-resources/open-protocol.html)).

## 2. Best sources (annotated links)

**Academic — selection theory and what survives:**
- Baks, Metrick & Wachter (2001, *JF*), *Should Investors Avoid All Actively Managed Mutual Funds?* — Bayesian result: even skeptical priors imply nonzero allocations to top managers ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=238513); [Wharton PDF](https://finance.wharton.upenn.edu/~jwachter/research/BaksMetrickWachter2001jf.pdf)).
- Pastor & Stambaugh (2002, *JFE*), *Mutual Fund Performance and Seemingly Unrelated Assets* — alpha/Sharpe estimated more precisely using non-benchmark assets; formalizes prior tightness ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=261117)).
- Barras, Scaillet & Wermers (2010, *JF*), *False Discoveries in Mutual Fund Performance* — FDR control; ~75% zero-alpha, ~24% unskilled, <1% skilled net of fees ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=869748); [MIT PDF](https://web.mit.edu/econometrics/fdr2version.pdf)). **Critical caveat:** Andrikogiannopoulou & Papakonstantinou (2019, *JF*) show FDR is low-powered and misclassifies large-alpha funds as zero ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3346034)); BSW reply ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3439231)). Read all three together.
- Berk & Green (2004, *JPE*) — equilibrium model: skill exists but net alpha is competed away via flows + decreasing returns to scale; explains why persistence is weak without implying no skill ([NBER w9275](https://www.nber.org/papers/w9275)). Extended by Berk & van Binsbergen (2015) value-added measure.
- Kosowski, Naik & Teo (2007, *JFE*), *Do Hedge Funds Deliver Alpha? A Bayesian and Bootstrap Analysis* ([RePEc](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jfinec/v84y2007i1p229-264.html)); Kosowski, Timmermann, Wermers & White (2006, *JF*) bootstrap for mutual-fund star-picking ([Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1540-6261.2006.01015.x)).

**Academic — measurement traps:**
- Getmansky, Lo & Makarov (2004, *JFE*) — illiquidity/return-smoothing produces serial correlation that inflates Sharpe and understates vol/beta; provides a smoothing-adjusted Sharpe ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=387578)).
- Goetzmann, Ingersoll, Spiegel & Welch — *Sharpening Sharpe Ratios* / Manipulation-Proof Performance Measure; Sharpe is gameable with optionality, MPPM is not ([NBER w9116](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w9116/w9116.pdf); [SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=302815)).
- Hedge-fund database biases (survivorship ~3%/yr, backfill/instant-history ~4%+/yr, self-selection) — combined biases account for much apparent alpha ([Breaking Down Finance summary](https://breakingdownfinance.com/finance-topics/alternative-investments/hedge-fund-database-biases/)).

**Academic — the allocator's own decisions:**
- Goyal & Wahal (2008, *JF*), *Selection and Termination of Investment Management Firms by Plan Sponsors* — sponsors hire on trailing outperformance and fire on underperformance; fired managers subsequently do as well as newly hired ones. The single most sobering source on allocator behavior ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=994304)).
- Jenkinson, Jones & Martinez (2016, *JF*), *Picking Winners?* — no evidence consultant recommendations add value; driven by soft factors; recommended (larger, higher-fee) managers underperformed non-recommended by ~1.1%/yr ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2327042); [PDF](https://www.umass.edu/preferen/You%20Must%20Read%20This/PickingWinners.pdf)). Follow-up *Virtual Reality?* documents inflated self-reported consultant track records ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3214693)).
- Fung, Hsieh, Naik & Ramadorai (2008, *JF*) — FoF value-add and capital-formation evidence ([SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=778124)); "Decreasing returns to scale and skill in hedge funds" (2023, *JBF*) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0378426623002005)).

**Allocator/industry primary sources:** NZ Super beliefs & evaluation pages (above); NBIM external-mandates history (above); SBAI toolbox and Open Protocol (above); CPP Investments external-PM insight (above).

## 3. White space

1. **Small-sample honesty is under-tooled.** With 36–60 monthly observations, the t-stat threshold for a true information ratio is punishing (IR > ~0.33 at 36m, ~0.25 at 60m for 95% confidence). Most allocator decks report point-estimate Sharpe/IR without confidence bands or FDR/multiple-testing adjustment across the manager set. A disciplined Bayesian-shrinkage layer (Baks–Metrick–Wachter / Pastor–Stambaugh applied to hedge funds) that reports *posterior* alpha with explicit priors is rare in public practice and is genuine white space.

2. **Smoothing correction as standard, not exception.** Getmansky–Lo–Makarov is 20 years old, yet most monitoring still uses raw reported Sharpe. Systematically de-smoothing every credit/less-liquid manager and flagging autocorrelation as a liquidity-risk *and* a mismarking red flag is under-deployed.

3. **Cross-sectional crowding/overlap analytics.** Public tooling for measuring manager-to-manager position overlap and factor-crowding (to catch diversification that is illusory and alpha that is decaying) is thin outside the largest quant shops — and is exactly what position-level transparency for "a few" managers enables.

4. **Decision audit against the Goyal–Wahal trap.** Almost no allocator publicly backtests its *own* hire/fire timing. An internal "would-we-have-been-better-off-holding-fired-managers?" scorecard is white space with immediate governance value.

5. **Tiered-transparency inference.** Formal methods for making the position-light majority comparable to the position-rich few — e.g., returns-based style/factor inference reconciled against Open Protocol exposure summaries where available — is an integration problem the public literature barely addresses.

## 4. Implications for idea cards

- **Bayesian shrinkage alpha with explicit priors → improves SELECT and SIZE.** Replace raw trailing alpha with posterior alpha (Baks–Metrick–Wachter / Pastor–Stambaugh), reporting the prior assumed and the posterior confidence interval. This directly disciplines both the go/no-go decision and the initial allocation weight, and resists the return-chasing that Goyal–Wahal shows destroys value.

- **De-smoothed, manipulation-aware performance panel → improves MONITOR and REDEEM.** For every manager, compute Getmansky–Lo–Makarov smoothing-adjusted Sharpe alongside the MPPM (Goetzmann et al.). A large gap between reported and adjusted metrics flags illiquidity/mismarking and feeds a redemption-review trigger.

- **Fung–Hsieh factor-attribution with alpha-significance gate → improves SELECT and ENGAGE.** Run every manager through the seven-factor (plus strategy-specific) model; if residual alpha's confidence interval crosses zero, the "alpha" is provisionally classed as alternative beta — cheaper to replicate. This both screens selection and arms engagement conversations about fees for beta.

- **FDR / multiple-testing overlay across the manager set → improves SELECT.** When ranking dozens of managers on t-stats simultaneously, apply Barras–Scaillet–Wermers-style false-discovery control — while explicitly noting its low power (Andrikogiannopoulou–Papakonstantinou) so it is used to *demote overconfidence*, not to auto-reject. Prevents luck being hired as skill.

- **Crowding/overlap and capacity monitor → improves SIZE and MONITOR.** Using the position-level tier plus factor proxies for the rest, measure inter-manager position overlap and per-strategy capacity/decreasing-returns signals. Rising overlap or AUM-driven alpha decay triggers a downsize before performance rolls over.

- **Own-decision audit scorecard → improves REDEEM and ENGAGE.** Institutionalize a Goyal–Wahal self-test: track post-termination performance of fired managers versus their replacements. If fired managers keep up, tighten the redemption bar and shift toward engagement/patience over rotation.

- **Tiered-transparency reconciliation layer (Open Protocol-aligned) → improves MONITOR.** Standardize the position-rich few into Open Protocol-style exposure buckets and use them to calibrate returns-based factor inference for the position-light majority, giving one comparable portfolio-level risk view across all transparency tiers.
