# Sweep E — Adoption Doctrine: Why PM-Facing Analytics Get Used or Shelved

## 1. State of practice

The evidence splits cleanly into two literatures that rarely cite each other: **academic work on why people reject quantitative advice**, and **vendor/practitioner work on packaging decision analytics for active equity managers**. Both matter for an allocator building manager-facing analytics under tiered transparency.

The academic core is *algorithm aversion*. Dietvorst, Simmons and Massey (2015) showed forecasters abandon a statistical model faster than a human after seeing either make the *same* error — confidence in the algorithm collapses on a single visible mistake even when it still beats the human. The follow-up (2018) is the more actionable result: people will use an imperfect algorithm if they can **modify it even trivially**; the effect is driven by a desire for *control*, not for accuracy, and holds when modifications are severely restricted. This is the single most important design constraint for PM-facing analytics — outputs must be adjustable, framed as inputs to the PM's judgment, never as a verdict. Adjacent advice-taking research (Bonaccio & Dalal 2006; the judge–advisor paradigm) documents *egocentric discounting*: decision-makers systematically overweight their own view. Discounting shrinks when advice comes from sources perceived as expert, experienced and confident, and when information is **framed as "advice" rather than as a competing opinion** — framing alone raises perceived helpfulness, trust and influence (Schultze/Loschelder line of work).

The substantive hook for a fundamental-manager program is the *selling* asymmetry. Akepanidtaworn, Di Mascio, Imas & Schmidt ("Selling Fast and Buying Slow," *Journal of Finance* 2023) analysed ~$570m-average institutional portfolios and found real skill in buying but selling that **underperforms a random-sell benchmark** — buys are slow and research-driven, sells are fast heuristic reactions to extreme returns. Because the deficit is concentrated, visible, and asymmetric, it is the ideal target for feedback: high-value, non-obvious, and not an indictment of the manager's core thesis work.

The commercial ecosystem operationalises exactly this. **Essentia Analytics** (Behavioral Alpha Benchmark, peer-reviewed in the *Journal of Investing*, Oct 2022, 90 equity PMs, seven decision "skills" from entry timing to exit timing) reports the recurring pattern that managers *accumulate* alpha through the book and then *give it back* through poor exit timing and biased size adjustments. Essentia's model is nudges + one-to-one coaching by ex-PMs + periodic reporting; it claims managers who engage with nudges outperform non-engagers by ~160 bps/yr, with a flagship case study citing ~4% incremental alpha and 435% software ROI (**vendor-published, treat as directional not independently verified**). **Cabot Investment Technology** (now FactSet) makes the parallel claim that buying skill and *buying-process consistency* predict future outperformance, with ~150 bps typical improvement over a client's first three years (**vendor-published**). **Inalytics** (Rick Di Mascio, a co-author of the *JF* paper) runs the DECSIS four-alpha-driver model — stock picking, sizing, trading, holding period — built on a large trade database, sold to asset owners (e.g. the former Towers Watson) for **manager selection and ongoing monitoring**, not just self-coaching.

The adoption/packaging literature is blunt about why this fails to land. McKinsey/QuantumBlack's "analytics translator" work names the *last-mile* gap — only ~18% of firms feel they use insights effectively, ~85% of data-science projects fail on business alignment (Gartner) — and prescribes a translator role that owns framing and adoption, not model-building. BI-adoption research is a cautionary mirror: average dashboard adoption sits near 25% (BARC), and the recurring cause is that **dashboards live in a separate tab, built for reporting rather than for a decision at a moment**. QBR practice shows the framing failure mode: a majority of recipients call quarterly reviews a waste of time when the deck is a generic template they could pull themselves. The consistent lesson across all three: analytics get used when they are *delivered into the workflow at the decision point, framed as help, from a credible translator* — and shelved when they are a standalone artifact that arrives on a cadence divorced from action.

Finally, the **trust/policing dynamic** has its own experimental base. Falk & Kosfeld's "Hidden Costs of Control" (*AER* 2006) shows that when a principal signals distrust through monitoring, agents *reduce* effort — control has hidden costs. For allocators this is directly load-bearing: analytics that read as auditing invite the manager to withdraw the very transparency (exposure detail, position files) the program depends on. Goodhart's law / metric-gaming rounds this out — any single behavioral score published as a target will be optimised for rather than improved. The managed-account/SMA literature (AIMA, Vidrio) frames the healthy version: transparency and risk guidelines set *collaboratively and contractually*, positioned as relationship-deepening, not surveillance.

## 3. White space

- **Multi-strategy behavioral analytics.** Essentia, Cabot and Inalytics are built almost entirely on *long-only/long-short equity* trade data. There is little public, rigorous work on decision-quality feedback for **macro, credit or quant** managers — where "positions" are less comparable and the sell/buy asymmetry may not hold. An allocator across strategies is largely in undocumented territory outside equity L/S. *(Flag: low-confidence that no such work exists privately; publicly it is thin.)*
- **Analytics under returns-only transparency.** Almost all documented decision analytics assume position- or trade-level data. What a quant team can credibly say to a manager from **monthly returns plus exposure summaries only** — and how to make *that* actionable rather than accusatory — is essentially undocumented publicly.
- **The allocator-as-coach loop.** Vendors serve managers directly (self-coaching) or serve allocators for *selection*. The specific engagement doctrine of an *external* allocator giving behavioral feedback *to a manager it does not employ* — without triggering the Falk-Kosfeld withdrawal — has no public playbook.
- **Counterfactual proof of "monetization."** Vendor ROI figures lack published counterfactuals. Rigorous evidence that a brief *changed* PM behavior and *produced* P&L (vs. selection effects — better managers buy the tool) is absent.

## 4. Implications for idea cards

- **Exit-timing / sell-discipline diagnostic (MONITOR + ENGAGE).** Build the flagship artifact around the *documented* deficit: alpha accrued through the book then given back at exit (SFBS 2023; Essentia). Frame as "where your edge leaks," not "where you're wrong." Highest-value, least-threatening entry point for engagement, and a monitoring signal that works even from exposure summaries.
- **Override-able, PM-adjustable outputs (ENGAGE).** Every model recommendation ships as a tunable input the PM can modify before it "counts" — directly applying Dietvorst 2018 to defeat algorithm aversion. Improves whether feedback is *acted on* rather than shelved.
- **Skill-vs-luck selection scorecard (SELECT + SIZE).** Use buying-process consistency and the four alpha drivers (Cabot/Inalytics/Essentia) to separate repeatable skill from lucky picks at hire, and to inform *conviction-weighted sizing* of the mandate. *(Confidence: methodology sound; predictive-power claims are vendor-published.)*
- **Behavioral-score deterioration as a soft redemption trigger (REDEEM).** A sustained fall in decision-quality (e.g. Essentia's below-50 signal) *plus* refusal to engage with feedback is an early, process-based redemption input that leads returns — used as a watchlist flag, never a mechanical rule (Goodhart risk).
- **Trust-preserving transparency ladder (ENGAGE + MONITOR).** Codify escalating data asks as collaborative and reciprocal (manager gets the analysis back), explicitly positioning analytics as help. Falk-Kosfeld predicts that framing this as audit will cause managers to *withdraw* position access — protecting the transparency tier is itself a design goal.
- **Kill the dashboard; deliver in-workflow via a translator (ENGAGE).** Replace the standing dashboard (25% adoption, separate-tab death) with a short, ex-PM-narrated PM pack or nudge tied to a specific upcoming decision and cadence (BI-graveyard + McKinsey translator evidence). Improves adoption of *any* monitoring output.
- **Sizing-discipline feedback (SIZE).** Isolate position-sizing skill (an independent alpha driver in DECSIS/Essentia) as its own card, giving managers feedback on scaling-in/out behavior — improving both the allocator's own sizing of the manager and the manager's sizing of positions.
